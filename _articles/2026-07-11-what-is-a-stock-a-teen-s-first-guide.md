# What is a Stock? A Teen's First Guide
### Difficulty: Beginner
### Tags: stocks, basics, fundamentals, beginner

## Introduction
Hey there, young investor! Are you curious about the world of stocks and investing? You're not alone. Many teens are starting to learn about personal finance and investing, and it's awesome that you're taking the first step. In this article, we'll break down what a stock is, why it matters to you, and how you can get started.

Imagine you own a lemonade stand, and you want to grow your business. You need money to buy more lemons, sugar, and cups. One way to get that money is by selling a part of your lemonade stand to your friends. In exchange, they become co-owners and get a share of the profits. This is similar to how stocks work.

## What Is It?
A stock, also known as equity, represents ownership in a company. When you buy a stock, you're essentially buying a tiny piece of that company. Stocks are traded on stock exchanges, like the New York Stock Exchange (NYSE) or NASDAQ.

Think of a company like a pizza that's divided into 10 slices. Each slice represents a share of the company. If you buy one slice, you own 1/10th of the pizza (or company). If the pizza becomes more valuable (the company grows), the value of your slice increases.

## Why Should Teens Care?
You might be wondering why you should care about stocks as a teen. Here's the thing: investing in stocks can help you achieve your long-term goals, like saving for college, a car, or even retirement. By starting early, you can take advantage of compound interest and potentially grow your money over time.

Plus, understanding stocks and investing can give you a head start in managing your finances and making informed decisions about your money.

## Key Concepts
Here are some key concepts to understand:

* **Stock price**: The current price of a single share of stock.
* **Share**: A unit of ownership in a company.
* **Portfolio**: A collection of stocks, bonds, or other investments.
* **Bull market**: A period of time when stock prices are rising.
* **Bear market**: A period of time when stock prices are falling.

Let's use an example to illustrate these concepts. Imagine you buy 10 shares of a company called "GameOn" at $10 per share. Your portfolio now includes 10 shares of GameOn stock. If the stock price rises to $15, your portfolio value increases to $150.
