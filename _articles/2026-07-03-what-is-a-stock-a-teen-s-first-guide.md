**What is a Stock? A Teen's First Guide**
=====================================================

**Difficulty: Beginner**
**Tags: stocks, basics, fundamentals, beginner**

**Introduction**
---------------

Imagine owning a part of your favorite company, like Apple or Nike. Sounds cool, right? Well, that's basically what a stock is. As a teenager, you might not be thinking about investing just yet, but trust me, it's essential to understand the basics of stocks. In this article, we'll break down what a stock is, why you should care, and some key concepts to get you started.

**What Is It?**
---------------

A stock, also known as equity, is a type of investment that represents ownership in a company. Think of it like a piece of a pizza. When you buy a stock, you're essentially buying a tiny slice of that company's assets and profits. Stocks are traded on stock exchanges, like the New York Stock Exchange (NYSE) or NASDAQ, where people can buy and sell them.

**Why Should Teens Care?**
-------------------------

You might be wondering why you should care about stocks as a teenager. Well, here's the thing: investing in stocks can help you achieve your long-term financial goals, like paying for college, a car, or even a house. By understanding how stocks work, you can start building wealth earlier and make informed decisions about your money. Plus, it's a great way to learn about business and economics in a real-world way.

**Key Concepts**
----------------

Here are some key concepts to understand about stocks:

* **Shares**: A share is a single unit of stock. When you buy a stock, you're buying a certain number of shares.
* **Stock price**: The price of a stock can fluctuate based on supply and demand. If a company is doing well, more people might want to buy its stock, which can drive up the price.
* **Dividends**: Some companies pay dividends, which are portions of their profits, to their shareholders.
* **Risk**: Investing in stocks involves risk. The value of your shares can go up or down, and there's a chance you might lose some or all of your investment.

**Real-World Examples**
------------------------

Let's say you buy 10 shares of Apple stock for $100 each. If the price of Apple stock goes up to $120, you can sell your shares for a profit. But, if the price drops to $80, you might lose money.

Another example is Nike. Let's say Nike has a great quarter and releases new, popular shoes. More people might want to buy Nike stock, which could drive up the price.
