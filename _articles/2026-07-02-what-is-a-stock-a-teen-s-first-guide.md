**What is a Stock? A Teen's First Guide**
=====================================================

**Difficulty: Beginner**
**Tags: stocks, basics, fundamentals, beginner**

**Introduction**
---------------

Imagine you own a lemonade stand. You make lemonade, sell it to thirsty customers, and keep the profits. Now, imagine your friend wants to help you grow your lemonade stand into a huge business. They offer to give you money to buy more lemons, sugar, and cups in exchange for a share of your business. This is similar to how stocks work. In this article, we'll explore what a stock is, why it matters to teens, and how it can help you achieve your financial goals.

**What Is It?**
----------------

A stock, also known as equity, is a type of investment that represents ownership in a company. When you buy a stock, you're essentially buying a tiny piece of that company. Stocks are traded on stock exchanges, like the New York Stock Exchange (NYSE) or NASDAQ.

Think of it like a pizza. Imagine a pizza with 10 slices. Each slice represents a share of the pizza. If you own one slice, you own 1/10th of the pizza. If the pizza becomes more valuable, the value of your slice increases.

**Why Should Teens Care?**
---------------------------

As a teen, you might not think that stocks are relevant to your life. But, understanding how stocks work can help you make informed decisions about your financial future. By investing in stocks, you can:

* Grow your savings over time
* Achieve long-term financial goals, like buying a car or paying for college
* Develop a sense of financial literacy and responsibility

**Key Concepts**
-----------------

Here are some key concepts to understand about stocks:

* **Share price**: The current price of one share of a company's stock.
* **Dividends**: Payments made by a company to its shareholders, usually quarterly or annually.
* **Earnings**: A company's profits, which can affect its stock price.
* **Risk**: The possibility that the value of your investment may decrease.

For example, imagine you buy a share of a company's stock for $100. If the company's earnings increase, the stock price might rise to $120. You could sell your share for a profit of $20. However, if the company's earnings decrease, the stock price might fall to $80. You could lose $20.

**Real-World Examples**
------------------------

Let's look at some real-world examples:

* **Amazon.com**: Amazon's stock price has increased significantly over the years, making it one of the most valuable companies in the world.
